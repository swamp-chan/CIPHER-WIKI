INTRODUCTION TO CRYPTOGRAPHY

Cryptography is the practice and study of techniques for secure communication. It involves creating and analyzing protocols that prevent unauthorized access to information.

Modern cryptography intersects mathematics, computer science, electrical engineering, and communication science. It forms the backbone of secure digital communication, protecting everything from online banking to private messaging.

Fundamentals

What is cryptology?

Cryptology is the comprehensive mathematical science encompassing both the design of secure, encrypted communication (cryptography) and the breaking of such codes (cryptanalysis)

The field is divided into two main branches: Cryptography focuses on designing secure communication systems, while Cryptanalysis focuses on breaking or finding weaknesses in these systems. Together, they form a continuous cycle of improvement in security techniques.

Understanding cryptology requires knowledge from mathematics, computer science, and information theory. It plays a crucial role in modern digital security, from protecting financial transactions to securing government communications.


What is cryptography?

Cryptography is the science of securing information through mathematical techniques. It enables parties to communicate securely over insecure channels, ensuring confidentiality, integrity, authenticity, and non-repudiation of data.

The word "cryptography" derives from the Greek words "kryptos" (hidden) and "graphein" (to write). Modern cryptography goes beyond simple secret writing to encompass complex mathematical operations that form the foundation of digital security.


History of Cryptography

Cryptography has been used throughout history for military and diplomatic purposes. Ancient civilizations used simple substitution ciphers, while medieval cryptographers developed more sophisticated polyalphabetic ciphers.


Core Principles

Cryptographic systems are built on fundamental principles: Confidentiality ensures only authorized parties can read the data. Integrity verifies data hasn't been tampered with. Authentication confirms the identity of parties. Non-repudiation prevents denial of actions.
